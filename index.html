Monday
Eastertide · Week Five

We are in the fifth week of Eastertide, the Monday after the Fifth Sunday of Easter.

This week the readings linger in the language of love, not as sentiment, but as the shape of life made possible by the risen Christ.

Word
LOVE
[luv]

Here is something that gets lost in translation.

In the New Testament, one of the words most often used for love is agapē (ἀγάπη).

It does not mean romance, affection or the ease we sometimes feel with those closest to us. It means a love that chooses the good of the other person, whether or not they are attractive, agreeable or easy to love.

When scripture says that God is love, it means that this kind of self-giving love is not only something God does. It is what God is.

Scripture

“Beloved, let us love one another, because love is from God; everyone who loves is born of God and knows God. Whoever does not love does not know God, for God is love.”

1 John 4:7–8 (NRSV)
Contemplation

Most of us find it easier to love when love comes naturally: when the person is kind, familiar or easy to be around.

But agapē begins where that ease begins to fail.

It is the love that does not depend on warmth, agreement or reward. To hold another person in agapē is not to pretend affection, but to seek their good before God when affection is no longer carrying you.

Who becomes harder for you to love when warmth, agreement or reward disappears — and what might it mean to seek their good before God?
Prayer

Lord,

Where our love depends on mood, warmth or convenience, deepen it.

Where we have drawn back from the person who costs us something, steady us.

Your love does not wait for us to become easy to love.

Teach us to love with something of your patience, your freedom and your constancy.

Let what we receive from you become what we offer to one another.

In Christ’s name, Amen.
Practice

Think today of one person you have recently found difficult to love.

Do one small, unadvertised good for them: answer more gently than you feel like answering, pray for them by name, or refuse one inward rehearsal of grievance.

Let it be something chosen, not something felt.

Love becomes more truthful when it is no longer waiting for ease.
Amen

Tuesday
Eastertide · Week Five

We are still inside the Farewell Discourse — Jesus speaking to the disciples in the upper room, with the cross a matter of hours away. Each day this week the words are given from that conversation, spoken to people who did not yet know they were the last.

Word
FRIEND
[frend]

In the ancient world, friendship was a moral and political category before it was a sentimental one. The Greek philos (φίλος) described someone bound to you by loyalty and shared life — not merely someone whose company you enjoyed.

In John 15, Jesus takes this word and overloads it. He calls the disciples no longer servants — who do not know what the master is doing — but friends, because he has told them everything the Father has given him to say. To be a friend of Christ is not a promotion. It is to be let into his confidence, handed his secrets, entrusted with his purposes. It is a weightier word than it sounds.

Scripture

"I do not call you servants any longer, because the servant does not know what the master is doing; but I have called you friends, because I have made known to you everything that I have heard from my Father." John 15:15 — NRSV

Contemplation

The move from servant to friend is not a relaxation of the relationship. A servant has clear boundaries — a defined role, a safe distance. A friend is let further in, and that is more exposed.

What Jesus is offering here is intimacy, not ease. He has told them everything. Not a selection, not what he judged they could handle, but everything the Father gave him to say. That is the nature of the friendship: it goes all the way down.

St Aelred of Rievaulx, the twelfth-century Cistercian, built an entire theology of spiritual friendship on this verse. He did not think it was a licence for sentimentality. He thought it was a vision of what human love could become when oriented toward Christ: the place where two people carry something of God toward each other without either of them needing to pretend.

What would it mean to receive the friendship of Christ — not as a comfort, but as a confidence?
Prayer

Lord,

You called us friends, and meant it.

Where we keep you at servant's distance — useful but not too close — draw us nearer.

Let your friendship make something of us: not merely consoled, but trusted, and changed.

We receive, in fear and in gratitude, everything you have made known.

In Christ's name, Amen.
Practice
Think of one person today toward whom your love has become effortful or thin — not a stranger, but someone close. Do not try to manufacture feeling. Instead, do one small, concrete act in their direction: a message, a patience, a kindness you would not naturally offer today. Agapē is always more verb than noun — chosen, willed, and returned to again and again, not from our own reserves, but from the source that does not run dry.
Amen

Wednesday
Eastertide · Week Five

Wednesday, still inside the week following the Fifth Sunday of Easter.

The Gospel of John continues to carry the week: the Farewell Discourse, in which Jesus prepares the disciples for a world in which he will no longer be visibly present. The words were first spoken in an upper room; now they are spoken into every season.

Word
CHOOSE
[chooz]

The Greek eklegomai (ἐκλέγομαι) means to select, to pick out, to set apart — with a deliberateness that implies both preference and purpose. The prefix ek- (out of) carries the sense of drawing one thing from among many.

In John 15, it is the word Jesus uses of his own initiative toward the disciples: I chose you. You did not choose me. This is not a statement about predestination in the speculative sense. It is a statement about priority and gift: the relationship did not begin with the disciples' seeking. It began with his.

The same word underlies the New Testament language of election — the sense, throughout Israel's history, that God's choice precedes and enables the human response.

Scripture

"You did not choose me but I chose you. And I appointed you to go and bear fruit, fruit that will last, so that the Father will give you whatever you ask him in my name." John 15:16 — NRSV

Contemplation

There is a kind of spiritual exhaustion that comes from believing the whole thing depends on us — that we are only in relationship with God as long as we keep reaching. The verse today quietly undoes that.

You did not choose me. It is not that the disciple's choice does not matter; it is that it was not first. Before any seeking, any turning, any prayer, there was a prior act: Christ's own movement toward the one he would call. This is not a theological abstraction. It is a pastoral reality — that on the days when we cannot feel that we have chosen God, or chosen well, or chosen at all, the relationship does not therefore end.

Jean Calvin, commenting on this verse, observed that election is not God's approval of what we already are. It is God's determination of what we will become. The fruit is not the condition of being chosen. It is the consequence.

On the days when you have not chosen well, what does it mean that you were chosen before you could?
Prayer

Lord,

You chose us before we had anything to offer.

On days when our faithfulness is thin and our love is inconsistent, remind us that the relationship does not rest on our choosing alone.

What you have appointed, sustain.

Bear fruit in us that we could not produce ourselves.

In Christ's name, Amen.
Practice

At some point today, recall a moment — recent or long past — when you were aware of being chosen: by a person, for a task, into a community. Notice what that felt like; notice how it changed the way you behaved in that context.

The life of faith is the practice of receiving a prior love — not earning it, but learning to live from it.
Amen

Thursday
Eastertide · Week Five

We are at the mid-point of the fifth week of Eastertide, and the Gospel readings continue from the Farewell Discourse and move into the High Priestly Prayer of John 17 — the longest sustained prayer in the Gospels, spoken by Jesus for his disciples on the night he was betrayed.

Word
INTERCEDE
[in-ter-seed]

To stand between — to speak on behalf of another before someone with the power to help. The Latin intercedere combines inter (between) and cedere (to go): to go into the space between two parties and carry one's petition there.

In the New Testament, intercession is what Christ does perpetually. The Letter to the Hebrews says he always lives to make intercession for those who approach God through him. John 17 is the great enacted instance: Jesus, hours before his death, praying not for himself but for those he is about to leave — and for all who will come to faith through their word.

Prayer for another is participation in what Christ is already doing.

Scripture

"I ask not only on behalf of these, but also on behalf of those who will believe in me through their word, that they may all be one." John 17:20–21a — NRSV

Contemplation

Jesus is praying for people who do not yet exist. "Those who will believe through their word" — that is us, among others, and every believer across the centuries who came to faith through the testimony of those first disciples. We are named, without being named, in this prayer.

The High Priestly Prayer is not a model for petition in the ordinary sense. It is a window onto what Christ is always doing: standing between us and the Father, speaking our names in a place we cannot reach ourselves. The Letter to the Hebrews says this is his permanent occupation — not just what he did in Gethsemane, but what he does, continuously, at the right hand of the Father.

To intercede for another, then, is to do something of what Christ is doing: to place yourself between a person and the one who can help them, and to speak. It is not magic and it is not manipulation. It is the act of love made speech.

Whose name are you carrying before God — and what does it cost you to stay in that place?
Prayer

Lord,

You intercede for us even now, even here.

Teach us to pray for one another with something of your persistence:

to carry names before God not once, but as a way of life. Hold those we bring today — the ones we are worried for, the ones we have almost given up on.

Let our intercession be a sharing in yours.

In Christ's name, Amen.
Practice

Choose one person to pray for today — specifically, not generally. Speak their name to God. Do not ask for a list of outcomes; simply bring the person: who they are, what they are carrying, what you cannot fix for them.

Intercession is less about changing God's mind than about placing someone in the light. You are not their advocate before an unwilling judge. You are bringing a name to a Father who already loves them.
Amen
