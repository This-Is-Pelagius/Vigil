Friday · Week Four · 1 May 2026
Optional Memorial ·

We are deep in Eastertide — the great fifty days between Easter and Pentecost, when the Church lives in the light of the resurrection.

Today the Church remembers — husband of Mary and earthly father of Jesus — whose memorial honours the quiet dignity of work done faithfully before God.

Word
ABIDE
[uh-byd]

To abide — from the Greek menō (μένω) — means more than staying put. It means remaining, dwelling, making a home. To abide is to make a home somewhere: the difference between being nearby and belonging within.

The word carries a domestic kind of closeness that English can miss. In 15, Jesus is not only telling his disciples where to stay. He is inviting them into a life lived from within his own — not standing at the threshold, but belonging in the house.

Scripture

Abide in me as I abide in you. Just as the branch cannot bear fruit by itself unless it abides in the vine, neither can you unless you abide in me. I am the vine, you are the branches. Those who abide in me and I in them bear much fruit, because apart from me you can do nothing.

John 15:4–5 (NRSV)
Contemplation

Notice what the branch is not asked to do first. Before it bears fruit — before it does anything at all — it abides. Abiding is not the whole of the Christian life, but it is where the rest of it begins. A branch cut off from the vine may still look alive for a while. But it will not bear fruit. To remain in Christ is to receive a life that is not self-made. Something begins to grow in you that you did not invent and cannot fully explain.

What would change if Christ were not only the one you admire, but the source of the life you live?
Prayer

Lord,

You ask us to abide in you, and you promise to abide in us.

Draw us deeper than mere nearness, into the kind of closeness that can bear your life.

Where we are trying to live on what we can produce alone, remind us that apart from you we can do nothing.

Let your life in us bear the fruit we cannot make by ourselves.

In Christ’s name, Amen.
Practice

At some point today, return to an ordinary task — washing up, making a coffee or tea, walking a familiar route — and pray quietly as you do it: abide in me, Lord, and let me abide in you. Let the prayer accompany the work, not replace it.

Fruit grows where life is received, not forced.

Saturday · Easter IV · 2 May 2026
Memorial · , Bishop and Doctor of the Church

We are in Eastertide — the season in which the Church learns what it is to live in the light of the risen Christ.

Today the Church remembers , whose defence of Christ’s full divinity still helps us hear the force of Jesus’ words: “Whoever has seen me has seen the Father.”

Word
KNOW
[noh]

In scripture, to know is often more than to understand. It is to recognise, to encounter, to stand in living relation to what is real.

In 14, Jesus is not speaking of knowledge as information. He is saying that to know him is to know the Father — not by collecting ideas about God, but by meeting the Father in the life, words and presence of Christ.

Scripture

If you know me, you will know my Father also. From now on you do know him and have seen him.

John 14:7 (NRSV)
Contemplation

We often behave as though knowing God meant getting the picture clearer: better concepts, better language, fewer mistakes. Some of that matters. But Jesus says something stranger here.

He does not give the disciples a fuller description of the Father. He gives them himself.

To know Christ is not only to think about him rightly, but to remain near enough that his life begins to teach you what God is like from within.

Where have you been asking God for a clearer explanation, when Christ is offering you himself?
Prayer

Lord,

You do not leave us with ideas about God, but bring us to the Father through yourself.

Where our faith has become distant, draw us nearer.

Where we want certainty more than communion, teach us to receive your presence.

Let our knowledge of you become more than thought: let it become trust, likeness and love.

In Christ’s name, Amen.
Practice

At one point today, when you feel the urge to demand a clear answer from God, stay quiet for a moment instead. Let that silence be a way of remaining with Christ until your need to master the moment begins to loosen.

Sometimes knowledge begins not with explanation, but with staying near.

5th Sunday of Easter
Eastertide · 3 May 2026

We are in Eastertide, on the Fifth Sunday of Easter — the season in which the life of the risen Christ begins to show itself in the life of his people.

Word
The Greek karpos (καρπός) means fruit: what is borne, what comes forth from a living thing in season.
[]

In scripture, fruit is not simply output or achievement. It is the visible sign of an inward life.

When St Paul speaks of the fruit of the Spirit, he names not what we can produce by ourselves, but what begins to appear when God's life is quietly at work in ours.

Scripture

But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, generosity, faithfulness, gentleness and self-control.

Galatians 5:22 (NRSV)
Contemplation

Most fruit does not announce itself while it is growing. It becomes visible later: in the patience that was not there before, the gentleness that now comes where sharpness once rose first, the peace that held when everything around you felt strained.

The work of the Spirit is often like that — quiet enough to miss if you are only looking for something dramatic. Which may be why the truest signs of grace are not always the ones we notice first in ourselves.

What fruit may already be growing in you, unnoticed because it does not look impressive?
Prayer

Lord,

Where your Spirit is at work in us quietly, teach us not to despise what is small or hidden.

Where love, faithfulness or generosity are beginning to grow, help us to recognise them with gratitude rather than pride.

Free us from the need to appear fruitful, and make us willing instead to be changed.

Let your life in us become visible in the ways that matter most.

In Christ’s name, Amen.
Practice

At some point today, notice one quiet sign of the Spirit’s fruit in someone near you: a kindness, a moment of self-control or an expression of joy that might easily go unremarked. Give thanks for it before God, whether or not you mention it aloud to them.

The ripest fruit is often that which draws the least attention.

Monday · Week Five · 4 May 2026

We are in the fifth week of Eastertide, the Monday after the Fifth Sunday of Easter. The resurrection light of the season is intensifying — and the readings of this week draw on the First Letter of John, written to a community learning what it means to live in the love that the risen Christ has made possible.

Word
LOVE
[luv]

Here is something that gets lost in translation. The ancient Greeks had at least four distinct words for love, where we have one. Philia for friendship. Eros for desire. Storgē for the love of family. And agapē (ἀγάπη) — the one the New Testament reaches for again and again — for something that has no real equivalent in everyday English at all.

Agapē is not a feeling. It is a direction. It moves toward another person — any person, including one you would rather avoid — and seeks their good, at cost to itself if necessary. It does not wait to be deserved. It does not switch off when the other person is difficult. In Greek thought, love moved upward, toward the beautiful and the worthy. Agapē moves the other way entirely.

This is the word the author of the First Letter of John uses when he writes that God is love. Not that God is loving, or that God loves — but that agapē is what God is. Not a property. A nature.

Scripture

Beloved, let us love one another, because love is from God; everyone who loves is born of God and knows God. Whoever does not love does not know God, for God is love.

1 John 4:7–8 — NRSV
Contemplation

Here is the thing about agapē that stops you short, once you see it.

The author of 1 John does not say: love one another, and God will love you back. He says: love one another because love is already from God — because agapē has already been given, and what we are being asked to do is pass on what we have received. The source is not in us. We are not being asked to generate something we do not have. We are being asked to conduct something that is already flowing.

Which changes the question entirely. The problem is not that we are not loving enough. It is that we keep forgetting where the supply comes from.

St Julian of Norwich spent twenty years sitting with this. Her answer, in the end, was disarmingly simple: the love does not run out. It cannot. Because it is not ours to begin with.

Where are you trying to love someone from your own reserves — and what would it mean to draw from the source instead?
Prayer

Lord,

You do not love us because we are loveable.

You love us because you are love, and you cannot be otherwise.

Where our love runs dry, replenish it from yours.

Teach us to love not from our own reserves, which are small, but from the inexhaustible source that made us.

In Christ's name, Amen.
Practice

Think of one person today toward whom your love has become effortful or thin — not a stranger, but someone close. Do not try to manufacture feeling. Instead, do one small, concrete act in their direction: a message, a patience, a kindness you would not naturally offer today.

Agapē is always more verb than noun — chosen, willed, and returned to again and again, not from our own reserves, but from the source that does not run dry.

Tuesday · Week Five · 5 May 2026

We are still inside the Farewell Discourse — Jesus speaking to the disciples in the upper room, with the cross a matter of hours away. Each day this week the words are given from that conversation, spoken to people who did not yet know they were the last.

Word
FRIEND
[frend]

In the ancient world, friendship was a moral and political category before it was a sentimental one. The Greek philos (φίλος) described someone bound to you by loyalty and shared life — not merely someone whose company you enjoyed.

In John 15, Jesus takes this word and overloads it. He calls the disciples no longer servants — who do not know what the master is doing — but friends, because he has told them everything the Father has given him to say. To be a friend of Christ is not a promotion. It is to be let into his confidence, handed his secrets, entrusted with his purposes. It is a weightier word than it sounds.

Scripture

I do not call you servants any longer, because the servant does not know what the master is doing; but I have called you friends, because I have made known to you everything that I have heard from my Father.

John 15:15 — NRSV
Contemplation

The move from servant to friend is not a relaxation of the relationship. A servant has clear boundaries — a defined role, a safe distance. A friend is let further in, and that is more exposed.

What Jesus is offering here is intimacy, not ease. He has told them everything. Not a selection, not what he judged they could handle, but everything the Father gave him to say. That is the nature of the friendship: it goes all the way down.

St Aelred of Rievaulx, the twelfth-century Cistercian, built an entire theology of spiritual friendship on this verse. He did not think it was a licence for sentimentality. He thought it was a vision of what human love could become when oriented toward Christ: the place where two people carry something of God toward each other without either of them needing to pretend.

What would it mean to receive the friendship of Christ — not as a comfort, but as a confidence?
Prayer

Lord,

You called us friends, and meant it.

Where we keep you at servant's distance — useful but not too close — draw us nearer.

Let your friendship make something of us: not merely consoled, but trusted, and changed.

We receive, in fear and in gratitude, everything you have made known.

In Christ's name, Amen.
Practice

Think of one person today toward whom your love has become effortful or thin — not a stranger, but someone close. Do not try to manufacture feeling. Instead, do one small, concrete act in their direction: a message, a patience, a kindness you would not naturally offer today. Agapē is always more verb than noun — chosen, willed, and returned to again and again, not from our own reserves, but from the source that does not run dry.

Wednesday · Week Five · 6 May 2026

Wednesday, still inside the week following the Fifth Sunday of Easter.

The Gospel of John continues to carry the week: the Farewell Discourse, in which Jesus prepares the disciples for a world in which he will no longer be visibly present. The words were first spoken in an upper room; now they are spoken into every season.

Word
CHOOSE
[chooz]

The Greek eklegomai (ἐκλέγομαι) means to select, to pick out, to set apart — with a deliberateness that implies both preference and purpose. The prefix ek- (out of) carries the sense of drawing one thing from among many.

In John 15, it is the word Jesus uses of his own initiative toward the disciples: I chose you. You did not choose me. This is not a statement about predestination in the speculative sense. It is a statement about priority and gift: the relationship did not begin with the disciples' seeking. It began with his.

The same word underlies the New Testament language of election — the sense, throughout Israel's history, that God's choice precedes and enables the human response.

Scripture

You did not choose me but I chose you. And I appointed you to go and bear fruit, fruit that will last, so that the Father will give you whatever you ask him in my name.

John 15:16 — NRSV
Contemplation

There is a kind of spiritual exhaustion that comes from believing the whole thing depends on us — that we are only in relationship with God as long as we keep reaching. The verse today quietly undoes that.

You did not choose me. It is not that the disciple's choice does not matter; it is that it was not first. Before any seeking, any turning, any prayer, there was a prior act: Christ's own movement toward the one he would call. This is not a theological abstraction. It is a pastoral reality — that on the days when we cannot feel that we have chosen God, or chosen well, or chosen at all, the relationship does not therefore end.

Jean Calvin, commenting on this verse, observed that election is not God's approval of what we already are. It is God's determination of what we will become. The fruit is not the condition of being chosen. It is the consequence.

On the days when you have not chosen well, what does it mean that you were chosen before you could?
Prayer

Lord,

You chose us before we had anything to offer.

On days when our faithfulness is thin and our love is inconsistent, remind us that the relationship does not rest on our choosing alone.

What you have appointed, sustain.

Bear fruit in us that we could not produce ourselves.

In Christ's name, Amen.
Practice

At some point today, recall a moment — recent or long past — when you were aware of being chosen: by a person, for a task, into a community. Notice what that felt like; notice how it changed the way you behaved in that context.

The life of faith is the practice of receiving a prior love — not earning it, but learning to live from it.

Thursday · Week Five · 7 May 2026

We are at the mid-point of the fifth week of Eastertide, and the Gospel readings continue from the Farewell Discourse and move into the High Priestly Prayer of John 17 — the longest sustained prayer in the Gospels, spoken by Jesus for his disciples on the night he was betrayed.

Word
INTERCEDE
[in-ter-seed]

To stand between — to speak on behalf of another before someone with the power to help. The Latin intercedere combines inter (between) and cedere (to go): to go into the space between two parties and carry one's petition there.

In the New Testament, intercession is what Christ does perpetually. The Letter to the Hebrews says he always lives to make intercession for those who approach God through him. John 17 is the great enacted instance: Jesus, hours before his death, praying not for himself but for those he is about to leave — and for all who will come to faith through their word.

Prayer for another is participation in what Christ is already doing.

Scripture

I ask not only on behalf of these, but also on behalf of those who will believe in me through their word, that they may all be one.

John 17:20–21a — NRSV
Contemplation

Jesus is praying for people who do not yet exist. "Those who will believe through their word" — that is us, among others, and every believer across the centuries who came to faith through the testimony of those first disciples. We are named, without being named, in this prayer.

The High Priestly Prayer is not a model for petition in the ordinary sense. It is a window onto what Christ is always doing: standing between us and the Father, speaking our names in a place we cannot reach ourselves. The Letter to the Hebrews says this is his permanent occupation — not just what he did in Gethsemane, but what he does, continuously, at the right hand of the Father.

To intercede for another, then, is to do something of what Christ is doing: to place yourself between a person and the one who can help them, and to speak. It is not magic and it is not manipulation. It is the act of love made speech.

Whose name are you carrying before God — and what does it cost you to stay in that place?
Prayer

Lord,

You intercede for us even now, even here.

Teach us to pray for one another with something of your persistence:

to carry names before God not once, but as a way of life. Hold those we bring today — the ones we are worried for, the ones we have almost given up on.

Let our intercession be a sharing in yours.

In Christ's name, Amen.
Practice

Choose one person to pray for today — specifically, not generally. Speak their name to God. Do not ask for a list of outcomes; simply bring the person: who they are, what they are carrying, what you cannot fix for them.

Intercession is less about changing God's mind than about placing someone in the light. You are not their advocate before an unwilling judge. You are bringing a name to a Father who already loves them.
